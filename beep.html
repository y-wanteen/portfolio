BEEP

OVERVIEW

Beep is a 3D action-adventure game about an alien with the ability to morph and adapt to its environment. After the alien's ship lands on a foreign planet, it must search for a new fuel source to power its spacecraft so that it can continue its journey through space.

SKILLS: Ideation, coding, debugging, playtesting

TOOLS: Unity3D, Adobe Illustrator, Autodesk Maya

TEAM: Nancy Chen, Danielle Ragas, Josephine Ryu

CONCEPT DEVELOPMENT

After deliberating on several other concepts, my team settled on one about a shape-shifting alien by drawing inspiration from the short animation we previously created together. We wanted the shape-shifting alien to travel through different biomes inhabited by aliens based on different geometric shapes. Depending on the shape of the aliens or flora imitated, it would gain different skills. We decided to focus on the first world inhabited by cubes given the time constraints as it was the most fleshed out.

PROGRAMMING

As the team’s main programmer, I coded the bulk of the game mechanics and features. Most notably this included the morphing ability and targeting system, the enemy AI, and the puzzle mechanics.

morph ability & targeting system

The morphing ability was the first thing I tackled as it was the crux of the game's concept. After finding that Unity’s built-in animation wouldn’t allow me to morph shapes, I used Maya to create a blend shape animation to export into Unity, making it to appear as if the alien would morph from a sphere into a cube and vice versa during a triggered event.

In order to trigger the actual morphing, the player had to be able to imitate something in the environment. We found that it would be too easy for the player to progress if they could imitate enemies through playtesting.

As a result, the morphing ability was restricted to special, imitable cube objects that allowed the player to morph into a cube shape when clicked. The targeting system's UI would let the player know what object they were targeting and a circular range around the player would give an indication for how close the player has to be to a cube in order to imitate it.

enemy ai

The AI for the cube enemy characters has three different states: patrolling the area, suspecting something is amiss, and chasing the player. This AI was refined several times over the course of development to include different reaction times and behaviours based on where the player was in the enemy's range of sight.

If the player managed to sneak up behind the enemy and hit them in the back, they would have successfully killed the enemy and be left with a corpse that would find use in later levels for puzzles.

A trapezoid enemy was later created by adapting the cube enemy’s AI. It essentially has all the same states, but also has an added state for sitting down. By hunkering down whenever the player tries to sneak up behind it, the trapezoid enemy becomes invulnerable and serves as a constant danger to the player.

Overall, the AI was quite challenging to make and required lots of playtesting, balancing and refining to make it challenging for players but not impossible for them to overcome.

puzzle mechanics & other

There were several puzzle mechanics that I programmed and attached to objects that the level design team later used when building out the levels. This included automated moving platforms and a number of different types of doors and switches. I had to rework the scripts for moving the doors and platforms several times to find a solution for the unstable frame rate and skipped frames that created bugs where a door wouldn't stay open or was eternally being pulled down by gravity.

Aside from this, other features I coded included the animation of the player alien blinking by manually switching through texture images in a script and optimizing the level loading by adding in a loading screen. I helped debug sound and UI features implemented by the other coder in the team as well.

REFLECTION

This was a fun game to make and also a great learning experience as it was the first time I had worked in Unity3D. I had to do a lot of research while implementing, testing, and optimizing features to our needs as they changed throughout development.

Overall I think we went a bit too crazy with making new levels. We should have narrowed things down to focus on polishing the levels we had instead of creating other levels that didn't get to go through as much playtesting and refinement before the final deadline.

Playtesting itself is definitely one of the things I found to be most important for the entire game making process. Having more outside testers near the deadline would have helped us pinpoint critical problem areas that needed to be addressed early on.
